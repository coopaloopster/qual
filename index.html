What is a string?

  • An extended, effectively 1-D object. Higher dimensional effects are suppressed by the width of the string.

Examples

  • Nielsen-Olesen Vortex: Solitonic object in Abelian-Higgs model
  • Cosmic String: Could have formed during phase transitions in early universe.
  • Fundamental Strings: Exactly 1D object, characterized by a tension, unifying GR and the Standard Model
  • QCD Strings: Flux tubes of glue, "experimentally" verified, that form between quark-antiquark pairs

Dynamics

$$ X^i(\sigma,\tau) \; \; , \; \; i \in \{2,3,\cdots,D\} $$

  • from the broken translation generators of Poincaré. σ and τ parameterize the 2 degrees of freedom of a scalar field confined to a string in spacetime.
  • The action will linearly realize $$ISO(1,1) \; \times \; SO(D-2)$$
  • In accordance with Goldstone's theorem, these scalar fields (w.r.t. worldsheet boosts) will be massless and hence described by a σ-model action with higher derivative terms suppressed by the width of the string.

Dynamics

  • If the underlying UV theory is Poincaré invariant, this larger symmetry must be non-linearly realized
  • Just like the CCWZ prescription for the chiral lagrangian, and Isham et al's spacetime generalization, we can connect this non-linear theory to a manifestly covariant theory

Dynamics

$$ (\sigma^{\alpha}, X^i) \rightarrow X^{\mu} $$

  • We bundle the world-sheet coordinates with the transverse fields into a D-dimensional spacetime vector which becomes the embedding coordinates of the worldsheet.

$$ \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{string}} = - \int d^2\sigma \sqrt{-\mathrm{det}(h_{\alpha \beta})} \left( l_s^{-2} + \frac{1}{\alpha_0} (K^i_{\alpha \beta})^2 + \cdots \right) $$

Dynamics

  • The sigma model action describing the Goldstone degrees of freedom can then be recovered by going into the static gauge.

$$ \tau \rightarrow X^0 \equiv t $$ $$ \mathcal{S}_{N.G.} \rightarrow -T_0 \int d^2 \sigma \sqrt{(1-|\partial_t \vec{X}|^2)(|\partial_{\sigma} \vec{X}|^2) + (\partial_{\sigma} \vec{X} \cdot \partial_t \vec{X})^2} $$-

  • A natural question to ask: does the manifestly covariant list of gemetric invariants, exhaust the possibilities for the effective non-linear theory?

Generic scaling zero variation:

Keeping track of these variations diagramatically and canceling them with higher order terms leads to the following prescription for generating non-linear invariance given a linearly invariant seed term:

The GM Procedure

$$ g_{ab} \equiv \eta_{ab} + \delta^{ij} \partial_a X_i \partial_b X_j \equiv \eta_{ab} + h_{ab} \\ t^{ij} \equiv \delta^{ij} - \partial_a X^i \partial_b X^j g^{ab} \\ \partial^3_{abc}X_i \rightarrow \nabla_{abc} X_i \equiv \partial^3_{abc} X_i - (\partial^2_{ab}X_j \partial_d X_k \partial^2_{ec} X_i g^{de} \delta^{jk} \\ + \mathrm{cyclic} \; \mathrm{permutations} \; \mathrm{of} \; abc)$$

Seed:                                        Seed: $$ \partial_a \partial_b X^k \partial^a \partial^b X_k \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \Box X^k \Box X_k \\ \rightarrow I_1 \;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \rightarrow I_2 $$

$$ I_1 = \sqrt{-g}(\partial_{ab}^2 X^k \partial_{cd}^2 X_k g^{ac} g^{bd} - \partial^2_{ab}X_k\partial^2_{cd}X_i\partial_e X^k\partial_f X^i g^{ac}g^{bd}g^{ef})$$ $$ I_2 = \sqrt{-g}(\partial_{ab}^2 X^k \partial_{cd}^2 X_k g^{ab} g^{cd} - \partial^2_{ab}X_k\partial^2_{cd}X_i\partial_e X^k\partial_f X^i g^{ab}g^{cd}g^{ef})$$

$$ I_1 - I_2 = \sqrt{-g} R $$ A simple calculation can be done, motivated by the Gauss-Codazzi relationship, to show that these invariants are accounted for in our linear Lagrangian by noting: $$ I_1 = (K_a^{ia})^2 \;\; \;\; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; I_2 = (K_a^{ib})(K_b^{ia})$$

..and higher order terms

Seed: $$ (\partial_a \partial^b X \cdot \partial_b \partial^c X)(\partial_c \partial^d X \cdot \partial_d \partial^a X) \\ \rightarrow I_3 $$ $$ I_3 = \sqrt{-g} t^{ij}t^{kl} \partial_{ab}^2 X_i \partial^2_{cd} X_j \partial^2_{ef} X_k \partial^2_{gh} X_l g^{ha} g^{bc} g^{de} g^{eg} $$ This is related to $$ \sqrt{-g} R_{ab} R^{ab} \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \mathrm{with} \; \; \; \; R_{ab} = g^{ef} t^{ij}(\partial^2_{ae} X_i \partial^2_{fb} X_j - \partial^2_{ab} X_i \partial^2_{ef} X_j) $$

Stückelberg fields for Diffeomorphisms

$$ \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{2} F_{\mu \nu}^{\dagger} F^{\mu \nu} + m^2 (A_{\mu}^{\dagger} - \frac{1}{m}\partial_{\mu} B^{\dagger})(A^{\mu} - \frac{1}{m} \partial^{\mu} B) \\ - (\partial^{\mu} A^{\dagger}_{\mu} + m B^{\dagger})(\partial_{\nu} A^{\nu} + m B) $$

$$ A_{\mu}(x) \rightarrow A_{\mu}(x) + \partial_{\mu}\Lambda(x) \\ B(x) \rightarrow B(x) + m \Lambda(x) $$

How can we apply this procedure to diffeomorphisms?

$$ \mathcal{S} = -M_{Pl}^2 \int d^4x \sqrt{-g}R + \int d^4 x \sqrt{-g} F $$

Introduce 4 fields corresponding to the broken diffs: $$ x^{\mu} \rightarrow \xi^{\mu}(x) $$ such that these fields admit the following equations of motion $$ \phi^{\mu}(x) = \Lambda^2 x^{\mu} \; \; \; g_{\mu \nu} = \eta_{\mu \nu} $$

$$ g_{\mu \nu} (x) \rightarrow \hat{g}_{\mu \nu} \equiv \frac{\partial Y^{\lambda}(x)}{\partial x^{\mu}}\frac{\partial Y^{\rho}(x)}{\partial x^{\nu}} g_{\lambda \rho} (Y(x))$$ $$ \hat{Y}: x^{\mu} \rightarrow Y^{\mu}(x)$$

The components of the diffeomorphism follow a strange transformation law, the components of the inverse diffs on the other hand behave exactly like a scalar. These are our desired goldstones $$ Y^{\mu} \rightarrow (\xi^{-1} \circ \hat{Y})^{\mu} \\ (Y^{-1})^{\mu} \rightarrow (\hat{Y}^{-1} \circ \xi)^{\mu} \therefore \phi^{\mu} \equiv (Y^{-1})^{\mu}$$

Future Directions

By exhausting posssible seed terms using the GM procedure, it seems that all operators compatible with the non-linear symmetry are in fact generated in the linear Lagrangian by the geometric invariants of the worldsheet. Our next task to start in the static gauge, and restore diffeomorphism invariance via the stückelberg fields, and see that this procedure does in fact realize D-dimensional Poincaré when the stückelberg fields are joined with the transverse fields in a D-dim multiplet.

Observables in quantum field theory must be gauge invariant objects since gauge invariance is just a redundancy of our description. This amounts to the corresponding Hilbert space operator commuting with the generator of gauge transformations. For GCT's not even a scalar field could be a local observable because it's variation implies that it's simply a constant field $$ \delta \phi \propto \epsilon^{\mu} \partial_{\mu} \phi \therefore \delta \phi = 0 \implies \partial_{\mu} \phi = 0 \; \; \forall \mu \in \{0,\cdots,D-1 \} $$

Although we don't have a control over the UV completion of gravity, low-energy local observables should still remain invariant w.r.t. the low energy gauge invariances.

$$ \mathcal{O} = \int d^4 x \sqrt{-g} \hat{\mathcal{O}}$$

An operator of this form is non-local but commutes with the constraints (the Wheeler-DeWitt equations)

$$ \mathcal{H} |\Psi\rangle = 0, \; \; \; \mathcal{H}_i |\Psi\rangle = 0 $$ This can be verified using the Heisenberg equations of motion for O $$ i \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \hat{\mathcal{O}}(x) = [\hat{\mathcal{O}}(x),\int_{\Sigma} (N\mathcal{H} + N^i \mathcal{H}_i)]. $$

Relational Observables

Our theory consists of sets of observables and massless scalars: $$ \mathcal{O}^a \; \; \; \; \; \; \; \; Z^i $$

We "instrument" our spacetime by preparing a background state in which the scalar fields take on a vev proportional to the spacetime coordinates. These play the role of our "clocks and rods". $$ \langle \Psi_Z | Z^i | \Psi_Z \rangle = \lambda \delta_{\mu}^i x^{\mu} $$

$$ O_{g,\xi} = \int d^4x \sqrt{-g} \mathcal{O}(x)e^{-\frac{1}{\sigma^2}(Z^i - \xi^i)^2} $$ This operator will serve as our pseudo-local operator. Notice that the integrand is localized around the point Z(x) = ξ with a resolution given by σ. The limiting case where σ goes to zero recovers a local operator.

A String Theory Calculation

$$ \langle \prod_i \mathcal{V_i} \rangle $$

We'll consider the simplest case, the tachyon ground state of the bosonic string. $$ \mathcal{V_i} = \int d^2 \sigma e^{i k_i x} $$

The Z-fields are chosen to be the background configuration of string with large target space field momentum and unit winding

$$ X^0 = p^0 t, X^1 = R \theta $$

and the observable in Gidding's et al calculation is the N-tachyon scattering amplitude.

$$ \mathcal{A}_{loc} \simeq \frac{1}{(4 \pi \sigma^2)^N} \int \left( \prod_{i=1}^N d^2 x_i e^{2 i t_i} e^{-(\frac{p^0}{\sigma})^2 (t_i - \hat{t}_i)^2 - (\frac{R}{2 \sigma})^2 (\theta_i - \hat{\theta}_i)^2} \right) \times $$ $$ f(x_i) \left\langle \prod_{i=0}^N \mathcal{O}_i(x_i) \right\rangle $$

Yielding the following result $$\Delta t \simeq \frac{\sigma}{p^0} \; \; , \; ;\ \Delta \theta \simeq \frac{\sigma}{R} $$ Corresponding to a target space bound on the resolution of our Z-fields.

Directions

  • The results of Giddings seem specious for several reasons but the technique of introducing relational observables could shed some light on the underlying physical intuition: that the physics of extended objects provides a natural cutoff for localization.
  • A direction to take this would be to use the before mentioned stückelberg technique to make a simple QFT, like a self-interacting scalar, diffeomorphism invariant and check that the σ → 0 limit in unaffected by this redundancy. Then try and understand how gravity/string theory disallows this limit dynamically from black hole production perhaps, or via transverse excitations of the string.
